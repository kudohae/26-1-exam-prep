Constraint
Anti-Pun Ordinance
Ch. 11 · RNR
A constraint that prohibits the pivot in Right-Node-Raising from being a homonym — i.e., a phonological form with two unrelated meanings. When the shared element in RNR has two distinct lexical meanings, the construction becomes unacceptable.

The Anti-Pun Ordinance captures the intuition that RNR requires genuine semantic identity between the pivot and its correlate in the other conjunct, not merely phonological identity. Because RNR under the LF Copy Analysis involves copying semantic content from the pronounced conjunct into the silent one at LF, a homonymous pivot fails: there is no single semantic object to copy.

Examples
Homonym as pivot — unacceptable
*John saw, and Mary sketched, the bank.
→ "bank" = financial institution OR riverbank — two lexical meanings
Unambiguous pivot — acceptable
John bought, and Mary read, the book.
Diagnostic value: Because the Anti-Pun Ordinance targets semantic identity rather than phonological form, it constitutes direct evidence that RNR involves LF-level identity — supporting the LF Copy Analysis over purely phonological accounts.
Anaphora
Deep Anaphora
Ch. 8 · Hankamer & Sag 1976
An anaphoric expression that is base-generated — it is not derived by a deletion transformation. Because no underlying syntactic structure is deleted, deep anaphors can be licensed by a pragmatic antecedent (a referent available in the non-linguistic situation) and do not require syntactic parallelism with a linguistic antecedent.
Diagnostic Properties
PropertyDeep AnaphorSurface Anaphor
Pragmatic antecedent✓ allowed✗ requires linguistic antecedent
Syntactic parallelismnot requiredrequired
Missing antecedent✗ blocked✓ allowed
Extraction from gap✗ impossible✓ possible
Canonical Example
do it — deep anaphor, licensed by situational context
[Speaker points at a heavy box]
A: "Can you lift this?"
B: "I'll try to do it."
→ No linguistic antecedent needed; the situation licenses "do it"
VP Ellipsis (surface anaphor) — pragmatic antecedent blocked
[Same situation]
*"I'll try to."
→ VPE requires a linguistic antecedent VP; situation alone is insufficient
Status of NCA: English NCA superficially patterns as a deep anaphor (allows pragmatic antecedent, no syntactic parallelism required) but also shows surface-anaphoric properties (missing antecedent blocked, extraction impossible). The textbook author therefore classifies English NCA as shallow anaphora — a third category distinct from both.
Theory
Default Case
Ch. 11 · RNR
A morphological case assigned to a nominal when no structural case (nominative, accusative) or inherent case (dative, genitive) is available — typically because the nominal is dislocated or stranded outside the clause that would normally license its case.

Default Case is relevant to RNR because the pivot, having been extracted (or copied) from both conjuncts, may no longer be within the case-licensing domain of either conjunct's verb or functional head. Languages differ in which morphological form serves as the default.

In the Context of RNR
Case mismatch at the pivot — illustrating the problem
Conjunct 1 expects accusative (direct object)
Conjunct 2 expects dative (indirect object)
John gave __ and Mary sent __ [the package] to Bill.
→ the pivot "the package" receives one surface form regardless
Theoretical relevance: Under movement-based analyses of RNR (Right-Node-Raising), the pivot escapes both conjuncts, so it cannot receive structural case from either. Default Case fills the gap. Under LF Copy Analysis, the pronounced conjunct assigns case normally; the silent copy at LF inherits case by identity — no default needed.
Ellipsis
Gapping
Ch. 7 · Ross 1970
A type of ellipsis in coordinate structures where the verb (or verb phrase) of the non-initial conjunct is deleted, leaving only the remnants (non-deleted constituents). Coined by Ross (1970).
Core Properties

Gapping requires: (i) a linguistic antecedent in the preceding conjunct, (ii) at least two remnants flanking the gap, (iii) the gap occupying the position of the verb.

Basic Gapping — English
John eats apples, and Mary Ø bananas.
→ "eats" deleted in second conjunct; "Mary" and "bananas" are remnants
Distinguishing Gapping from VP Ellipsis
Gapping: John ate apples and Mary Ø pears. (no auxiliary survives)
VPE:     John ate apples and Mary did Ø too. (auxiliary "did" survives)
Relation to Korean Focus Ellipsis

Ch. 7 treats Korean focus constructions that resemble pseudogapping (remnant + focus auxiliary i) as distinct from canonical Gapping. In Korean, Gapping-like deletion is licensed by the [+focus] feature on the remnant, which moves to Spec-FocP before the ellipsis site is deleted at PF.

Key contrast with Pseudogapping: Gapping has no auxiliary; Pseudogapping has an auxiliary. Both have a post-gap remnant; VP Ellipsis has no remnant.
Semantics
Idiom Spreading
= Idiom Substitution
Ch. 7–8 · Rottman & Yoshida 2013
The phenomenon whereby an idiomatic reading is preserved even when part of the idiomatic string is deleted under ellipsis (or co-indexed with an anaphor). The idiom's meaning "spreads" into the ellipsis site or is "substituted" by the anaphoric expression without loss of the non-compositional meaning.

Idioms must form a constituent at LF to receive their idiomatic interpretation. If ellipsis deletes part of an idiom yet the idiomatic reading survives, this constitutes evidence that the deleted material is structurally present at LF — i.e., the construction involves genuine ellipsis (with a syntactic structure that is merely unpronounced) rather than a base-generated zero element.

Diagnostic Use
Sluicing preserving idiomatic reading — Rottman & Yoshida 2013
Someone kicked the bucket, but I don't know who Ø.
→ "kick the bucket" = die (idiomatic). The sluiced gap preserves this reading.
→ Proves the deleted TP contains [kicked the bucket], i.e., ellipsis has internal structure.
NCA preserving idiomatic reading — Ch. 8
A: Did you kick the bucket? B: I refused Ø.
→ NCA preserving idiomatic interpretation of the complement
Contrast: overt pronoun blocks idiomatic reading
*Someone kicked the bucket, but I don't know who did it.
→ "it" forces a literal reading (no idiomatic reading available)
Core argument: Idiom spreading proves that the ellipsis site has the same syntactic structure as its antecedent at LF, even though that structure is phonologically silent at PF.
Semantics
Lexical Decomposition
Ch. 8 · NCA
The analysis of a word's meaning into more primitive semantic components or sub-events. For example, kill = cause to become dead; disapprove = not approve. Relevant to NCA because it determines what counts as the semantic complement of a predicate.

In Ch. 8, lexical decomposition is invoked to explain why certain predicates permit NCA while others do not. The complement that NCA targets is not always a syntactically overt CP or DP — it may be a propositional argument that is encoded in the predicate's lexical semantic structure.

Application to NCA
Approve — NCA licensed
A: I eat meat and go to bed late.
B: My brother doesn't approve Ø.
→ The null complement = the proposition described by A's utterance
Disapprove — NCA blocked despite similar meaning
*My brother disapproves Ø.
→ "disapprove" lexically decomposes as [not approve], but the resulting
syntactic frame does not license a null complement in the same way
Key point: NCA is lexically determined. It is not enough for two predicates to be semantically equivalent — the specific lexical item must independently license a null complement slot. Lexical decomposition explains why near-synonyms diverge in NCA-licensing.
Theory
LF Interpretive Parallelism
Ch. 8, 11
The condition that ellipsis (and some forms of anaphora) requires the logical form of the ellipsis site to be identical (or sufficiently parallel) to the logical form of its antecedent. Syntactic surface identity is not required; what matters is identity at the level of semantic interpretation.

LF Interpretive Parallelism underlies the LF Copy Analysis of RNR (Ch. 11): the silent conjunct's LF content is copied from the pronounced conjunct, ensuring interpretive identity. It also motivates the distinction between strict and sloppy readings in VP Ellipsis, since both are compatible with LF identity given different binding relations.

Strict vs. Sloppy in Terms of LF Parallelism
VP Ellipsis — both readings satisfy LF parallelism
John washed his car, and Bill did Ø too.
Strict: Bill washed John's car — LF of gap = [washed John's car]
Sloppy: Bill washed Bill's car — LF of gap = [washed Bill's car]
→ Both are valid LF copies of the antecedent VP under different binding
Anti-Pun Ordinance as a consequence of LF Interpretive Parallelism
*John robbed, and Mary painted, the bank.
→ No single LF can be copied: the two uses of "bank" have different LFs
Contrast with surface (phonological) identity: The LF Interpretive Parallelism condition allows vehicle change (e.g., pronoun ↔ reflexive alternations) and sloppy readings that pure phonological identity would rule out.
Theory
Locative Inversion
Ch. 7 · Pseudogapping
A construction in which a locative phrase fronts to subject position and the canonical subject moves postverbally. Relevant to pseudogapping because locative PPs can surface as remnants in pseudogapping contexts, which Lasnik's (1995) movement-based analysis struggles to accommodate.
Canonical Locative Inversion
Into the room walked a stranger.
→ PP "into the room" in subject position; "a stranger" postverbal
Locative PP as pseudogapping remnant — the problem
A: John put the book on the shelf.
B: Mary did Ø on the table.
→ The PP "on the table" is a locative remnant in pseudogapping
→ Lasnik's Object Shift analysis predicts only DP objects can be remnants,
yet PP remnants are attested — a challenge to movement-based accounts
Theoretical relevance: The availability of locative PP remnants in pseudogapping suggests that the remnant is not necessarily the result of overt DP movement (Object Shift). Alternative analyses (e.g., VP-internal deletion) must be invoked to handle PP remnants.
Ellipsis
Not-Stripping
Ch. 9–10
A subtype of stripping in which the remnant is accompanied by not (or a negative particle), expressing contrastive negation — the remnant is contrasted with the antecedent by being negated.

Not-stripping is the counterpart of Why-stripping: while Why-stripping asks why the antecedent proposition holds, Not-stripping denies it or contrasts the remnant with an element in the antecedent. The remnant in Not-stripping is a focus-moved element (moving to Spec-FocP), not a topic-moved one.

Core Examples
Not-Stripping
A: John ate natto.
B: Not NATTO (but sushi).
→ Contrastive: "what John ate was not natto (but something else)"
Island sensitivity — Not-Stripping cannot rescue island violations
A: No linguist recommended a book that contains one of his own articles.
*B: Not HIS OWN ARTICLES.
→ The remnant inside an island cannot escape; island violation not rescued

B: Why his own articles? ← Why-stripping rescues the same island violation
Why Not-Stripping Cannot Rescue Islands

Under the author's analysis, Not-stripping remnants undergo focus movement (to Spec-FocP). Focus-moved elements are not D-linked; they introduce genuinely new information and therefore cannot leave a resumptive pronoun. Without resumption, the island violation is not rescued.

Contrast with Why-Stripping: Why-stripping remnants undergo topic movement (D-linked, old information) → island rescue possible. Not-stripping remnants undergo focus movement (non-D-linked) → island rescue impossible.
Anaphora
Null Complement Anaphora
Ch. 8
A construction in which a verb appears without its expected complement, yet the missing complement is interpreted via a contextually available antecedent. NCA is lexically restricted: only certain predicates (e.g., approve, refuse, try, volunteer, forget, inquire) license a null complement.
Key Examples
NCA-licensing verbs
A: I eat meat and go to bed late.
B: My brother doesn't approve Ø. ✓ approve licenses NCA
B: I've tried to stop Ø. ✓ try licenses NCA
B: I refused Ø. ✓ refuse licenses NCA
Non-NCA-licensing verb
A: Mary believes that Anne is pregnant.
B: *But I don't believe Ø. ✗ believe does not license NCA
B: But I don't believe it. ✓ overt pronoun required
B: But I don't believe so. ✓ propositional pro-form
Four Diagnostic Properties (Hankamer & Sag 1976 / Depiante 2000)
PropertyEnglish NCAPrediction
Pragmatic antecedent✓ alloweddeep anaphor
Syntactic parallelismnot requireddeep anaphor
Missing antecedent✗ blockedsurface anaphor
Extraction from gap✗ impossiblesurface anaphor
Missing Antecedent Test
VP Ellipsis — missing antecedent allowed (surface anaphor)
I've never ridden a camel, but Ivan has Ø and he says it stank horribly.
→ "it" corefers with "a camel" inside the deleted VP — missing antecedent OK
NCA — missing antecedent blocked
*Jack didn't cut Betty with a knife. Bill did it [NCA], and it was rusty.
→ "it" cannot pick up "a knife" from inside NCA's null complement
Classification
English NCA = Shallow Anaphora (author's proposal): base-generated null pro-form, not derived by deletion. Patterns as deep anaphor in tests 1–2, as surface anaphor in tests 3–4. Not purely either.
Korean NCA = Deep Anaphora: realized as a null pro-form; allows both strict and sloppy readings regardless of whether the binder is a name [+α] or pronoun [+β] — the hallmark of deep anaphora under Hoji (2003).

Pivot
Ch. 11–12 · RNR / LNS
The shared constituent in Right-Node-Raising (or Left-Node-Sharing) that is phonologically realized in only one of the conjuncts while being semantically interpreted in both.
RNR — pivot pronounced in right conjunct
John bought, and Mary read, [the book].
→ pivot = "the book"; silent in left conjunct, pronounced in right
LNS — pivot pronounced in left conjunct
[the sandwich] John made, and Mary ate.
→ pivot = "the sandwich"; pronounced in left conjunct, silent in right
Analytic Status of the Pivot
AnalysisStatus of silent pivot
Right-Node-Raising (movement)Trace / copy left behind after ATB movement
Multiple Dominance (Wilder 1999)Same node dominated by two mothers
Backward DeletionDeleted copy in left conjunct (base-generated in right)
LF Copy AnalysisPhonologically null; semantic content copied from pronounced conjunct at LF
Anti-Pun Ordinance restricts the pivot: a pivot that is a homonym (two unrelated lexical meanings) is banned, since no single LF can be copied for both conjuncts.
Constraint
Proximity Effect
Ch. 11 · RNR
The observation that in Right-Node-Raising, agreement (especially honorific agreement in Korean) on the pivot is determined by the nearest conjunct — i.e., the right conjunct — rather than by the left (gap) conjunct.

In Korean RNR, the honorific morpheme -si- on the pivot verb reflects the subject of the right conjunct, not the subject of the left (elided) conjunct. This is the proximity effect: the pronounced element controls agreement because it is adjacent to (proximate to) the pivot.

Honorific agreement controlled by right conjunct
Left conjunct subject: non-honorific referent
Right conjunct subject: honorific referent (e.g., professor)
→ Pivot verb takes honorific -si- (right conjunct controls)

Left conjunct subject: honorific referent
Right conjunct subject: non-honorific
→ Pivot verb without -si- is degraded
Theoretical import: The proximity effect supports the LF Copy Analysis: the pivot is base-generated in the right conjunct and its morphological features are determined there. The left conjunct contains a phonologically null copy whose LF content is imported from the right.
Ellipsis
Pseudogapping
Ch. 7 · Levin 1978
An ellipsis construction combining properties of both VP Ellipsis and Gapping: an auxiliary survives (like VPE) and a post-auxiliary remnant remains (like Gapping). Named by Levin (1978).
Pseudogapping
A: John eats apples.
B: Mary does Ø bananas.
→ "does" = auxiliary survives; "bananas" = remnant; VP deleted
Three-Way Contrast
ConstructionAuxiliaryRemnantExample
Gapping✗✓John ate apples and Mary Ø pears.
VP Ellipsis✓✗John ate apples and Mary did Ø too.
Pseudogapping✓✓John ate apples and Mary did Ø pears.
Lasnik's (1995) Analysis

Lasnik derives pseudogapping from Overt Object Shift: the remnant object moves out of VP before VP ellipsis applies. The auxiliary survives because it is outside the ellipsis domain; the remnant survives because it has already moved out.

Lasnik's derivation (simplified)
Step 1: Object "bananas" undergoes Object Shift → moves above VP
Step 2: VP (containing the trace of "bananas") is deleted
Result: [TP Mary [does [bananasi [VP Ø]]]]
Problem for Lasnik: Locative PP remnants are also attested in pseudogapping contexts, but PPs do not undergo Object Shift. A purely movement-based account cannot straightforwardly handle PP remnants.
Ellipsis
Right-Node-Raising
Ch. 11
A coordinate construction in which a shared constituent (the pivot) is pronounced only in the rightmost conjunct while being semantically interpreted in all conjuncts. Also called Right-Node-Sharing (RNS).
Basic RNR
John bought Ø, and Mary read, [the book].
→ "the book" interpreted in both conjuncts; pronounced only in second

Syntax
Right Dislocation
Ch. 13
A construction consisting of a host clause containing a gap (or pronoun) and an appendix — a dislocated constituent at the right periphery — co-indexed with that gap/pronoun.
English Right Dislocation — pronoun in host
She's a brilliant linguist, [Mary].
→ host contains pronoun "she"; appendix = "Mary"
Korean Right Dislocation — gap in host
Yesterday I met Ø, [Suni].
→ host contains a gap (not a pronoun); appendix = "Suni"
→ Key typological difference from English
Competing Analyses
AnalysisResearchersKey Claim
Single-clause movementChoi (1987)Appendix moves rightward from host
Single-clause base-generationSells (1999), LeeEvent projection E licenses both
Double-clause base-generationLee (2009, 2010)Two independent clauses; island effects = processing difficulty
Double-clause movement + deletionChung (2009)Appendix moves left; TP deleted in appendix clause
Mixed analysisKo (2015)Argument appendix: focus movement + topic movement; adverb appendix: concatenation/sideward movement
Key Properties from Survey Data
Argument–Adjunct Asymmetry: Adjunct appendices yield higher acceptability than argument appendices — consistent with Ko (2015)'s mixed analysis.
Island sensitivity: Korean RD respects island constraints → movement is involved; double-clause base-generation analysis (which attributes island effects to processing) is undermined.
Resumptive pronoun: When a resumptive pronoun appears in the host gap position, acceptability decreases — the gap (not a pronoun) is the canonical form, unlike English.
Author's conclusion: Ko (2015)'s mixed analysis is most promising; Choi (1988)'s rightward movement analysis remains a viable alternative.
Semantics
Sloppy Reading
Ch. 8 · Fiengo & May 1994
In VP Ellipsis (and related ellipsis constructions), the reading in which a pronoun inside the ellipsis site is interpreted as co-varying with the new subject of the ellipsis clause — i.e., the pronoun takes a new referent rather than the original one. Corresponds to a β-occurrence (dependent occurrence) in Fiengo & May (1994).
Sloppy reading in VP Ellipsis
George claimed he won. Al did Ø too.
Sloppy: Al claimed Al won. (pronoun = Al, not George)
Strict: Al claimed George won. (pronoun = George)
Fiengo & May (1994): α- vs. β-Occurrence
α-occurrence (independent) → strict reading only
The pronoun's index is an independent token → copied as-is → strict only
John washed John's car. Bill did Ø too.
Strict: Bill washed John's car.
Sloppy: *Bill washed Bill's car. (name = α-occurrence, no sloppy)
β-occurrence (dependent) → sloppy reading available
The pronoun's index depends on the subject → copied as dependency → sloppy possible
John washed his car. Bill did Ø too.
Strict: Bill washed John's car.
Sloppy: Bill washed Bill's car.
Relevance to NCA

Korean NCA allows sloppy readings even when the binder is a name (α-occurrence) — behavior inconsistent with surface anaphora and diagnostic of deep anaphora (Ch. 8).

Ellipsis
Sluicing
Ch. 9 · Ross 1969
An ellipsis construction in which a wh-expression moves to the left periphery of an embedded clause and the remainder of that clause (TP) is deleted. The result is a bare wh-remnant. Coined by Ross (1969).
Basic Sluicing
A: Someone left early.
B: I wonder who Ø.
→ "who" = wh-remnant; deleted TP = "who left early"
Merger Sluicing and Island Rescue
Island rescue via Sluicing (Merger Sluicing)
A: She's dating someone, but I don't know who Ø.
→ Antecedent "someone" is inside an island (existential NP)
→ Sluicing rescues the island violation: *-marked trace deleted by TP deletion
Idiom Spreading in Sluicing — Rottman & Yoshida (2013)
Someone kicked the bucket, but I don't know who Ø.
→ Idiomatic reading ("die") preserved in sluice → proves TP has full structure at LF
P-Stranding Generalization (Merchant 2001)
English — P-stranding allowed → sluicing without P also allowed
She's talking to someone, but I don't know who Ø. (P stranded)
German — P-stranding not allowed → sluicing requires P pied-piping
Sie spricht mit jemandem, aber ich weiß nicht mit wem Ø.
"She's talking with someone, but I don't know with whom Ø."
Relation to Why-Stripping: Both involve wh-movement + TP deletion. Why-Stripping is a non-interrogative counterpart where why is generated in C and the remnant undergoes topic (not wh-) movement.
Semantics
Strict Reading
Ch. 8 · Fiengo & May 1994
In VP Ellipsis (and related ellipsis constructions), the reading in which a pronoun inside the ellipsis site is interpreted as co-referring with the same individual as in the antecedent clause — the pronoun retains its original referent. Corresponds to an α-occurrence (independent occurrence) in Fiengo & May (1994).
Strict reading in VP Ellipsis
John washed his car. Bill did Ø too.
Strict: Bill washed John's car. (his = John)
Only strict reading available — name as binder (α-occurrence)
John washed John's car. Bill did Ø too.
Strict only: Bill washed John's car.
*Sloppy: Bill washed Bill's car.
→ "John's" = α-occurrence; sloppy reading unavailable
Relevance to NCA Classification
English NCA — strict reading available (as expected for deep anaphor)
Mary voted for her husband. Susan refused Ø.
Strict: Susan refused to vote for Mary's husband.
Sloppy: Susan refused to vote for Susan's husband.
→ Both available → NCA patterning as deep anaphor here
Key distinction: Strict reading is available for both surface and deep anaphors. The critical diagnostic is whether the sloppy reading is available when the binder is a name — only deep anaphors allow this.
Ellipsis
Stripping
Ch. 7, 9, 10 · Hankamer & Sag 1976
An ellipsis construction in which all of the non-initial conjunct is deleted except for a single remnant. Structurally, stripping = TP deletion with one element (the remnant) having escaped deletion, typically by movement to the left periphery.
Basic Stripping (= Bare Argument Ellipsis)
A: John left early.
B: Mary too Ø. / B: Not John Ø.
→ Single remnant + TP deletion
Subtypes
SubtypeRemnant TypeMovement of Remnant
Plain StrippingFocus XP (+ too / either)Focus movement (Spec-FocP)
Not-StrippingFocus XP + notFocus movement
Why-StrippingXP + whyTopic movement (Spec-TopP) — author's claim
Stripping in Korean — maximization of ellipsis
A: Chulsoo ate apples.
B: Younghee too Ø. [z = 1.15] — TP stripping, highest preference
→ Maximization principle: larger ellipsis preferred over smaller
TP deletion > NegP deletion > AgrSP deletion > VP deletion
Structural analysis: The remnant moves to Spec-FocP (or Spec-TopP for Why-Stripping) before the TP is deleted at PF. The [+focus] feature on the remnant forces its movement, satisfying the Recoverability Condition (Pesetsky 1997).
Anaphora
Surface Anaphora
Ch. 8 · Hankamer & Sag 1976
An anaphoric expression derived by a deletion transformation. The deleted material must have a syntactically appropriate linguistic antecedent; a pragmatic antecedent alone is insufficient. Surface anaphors are sensitive to syntactic parallelism.
Diagnostic Properties
PropertySurface AnaphorDeep Anaphor
Pragmatic antecedent✗ blocked✓ allowed
Syntactic parallelismrequirednot required
Missing antecedent✓ allowed✗ blocked
Extraction from gap✓ possible✗ impossible
Canonical Examples
VP Ellipsis — surface anaphor, pragmatic antecedent blocked
[Speaker lifts a heavy box without being asked]
*A: "Can you lift this?" B: "I already did Ø."
→ VPE requires prior linguistic antecedent VP, not situational context alone
VP Ellipsis — missing antecedent allowed
I've never ridden a camel, but Ivan has Ø and he says it stank horribly.
→ "it" co-refers with "a camel" inside the deleted VP — missing antecedent OK
VP Ellipsis — extraction possible (surface anaphor)
I know which book Mary read t, and Peter knows which article Sally did Ø.
→ Extracting from within the elided VP is grammatical
VP Ellipsis, do so anaphora, and sluicing are the canonical surface anaphors. English NCA, despite sharing some deep-anaphoric properties, behaves as a surface anaphor in the missing-antecedent and extraction tests.
Constraint
Syntactic Parallelism
Ch. 8
The requirement that the ellipsis site and its antecedent share the same (or sufficiently similar) syntactic structure. A surface anaphor is licensed only when the antecedent VP/TP has a matching syntactic form — paraphrase or semantic equivalence alone is insufficient.
VP Ellipsis — syntactic parallelism required
A: The oats had to be taken to the bin.
*B: Bill did Ø. (antecedent is passive; VPE site expects active)
B: Bill volunteered Ø. (NCA — parallelism not required)
Do so — strict syntactic parallelism
A: John solved the problem carefully.
B: Mary did so too. (identical VP structure)
B: *Mary did so the puzzle. (structure mismatch)
NCA is Exempt
NCA — syntactic parallelism not required
A: The oats had to be taken to the bin.
B: Bill volunteered Ø.
→ "volunteered" takes a null complement regardless of A's syntactic structure
→ Parallelism exemption = evidence that NCA is not a surface anaphor
Diagnostic: Sensitivity to syntactic parallelism is the hallmark of surface anaphora. Immunity to it is the hallmark of deep anaphora. NCA's immunity to parallelism was Hankamer & Sag's original motivation for classifying NCA as deep anaphora — though later tests (missing antecedent, extraction) complicate this.
Ellipsis
VP Ellipsis
Ch. 7
An ellipsis construction in which the entire VP is deleted while the auxiliary survives. The deleted VP must have a syntactically appropriate antecedent in the discourse (surface anaphor).
Basic VP Ellipsis — English
A: John eats apples.
B: Mary does Ø too.
→ "does" = auxiliary survives; VP "eats apples" deleted
Strict and sloppy readings
John washed his car. Bill did Ø too.
Strict: Bill washed John's car.
Sloppy: Bill washed Bill's car.
Missing antecedent — VPE allows it (surface anaphor)
I've never ridden a camel, but Ivan has Ø and he says it stank horribly.
→ "it" = "a camel" inside the deleted VP — missing antecedent rescued
Pragmatic antecedent — VPE blocks it
[Speaker gestures at a box]
*A: "Lift this?" B: "I already did Ø."
→ No linguistic antecedent VP; VPE requires one
In Korean

Korean lacks a morphological auxiliary of the English type. Korean VPE-like constructions in focus sentences require insertion of the focus copula i as a last resort — e.g., -do ya constructions where VP is deleted under focus. The [+focus] feature drives the remnant to Spec-FocP, and the copula i is inserted to support the surviving TP.

Surface anaphor status: VPE requires a linguistic antecedent, is sensitive to syntactic parallelism, allows missing antecedents, and allows wh-extraction from the gap — all hallmarks of surface anaphora (Ch. 8).
Ellipsis
Why-Stripping
Ch. 9–10 · Yoshida et al. 2015
A construction in which why appears at the left periphery and a single XP remnant is pronounced, with the rest of the clause (TP) deleted. The remnant is an element from the antecedent proposition that the speaker questions the reason for. Described and named by Yoshida et al. (2015).
Basic Why-Stripping
A: John was eating natto.
B: Why natto (and not sushi)?
→ "why" in C; "natto" = remnant; TP "[John was eating t]" deleted
Three Key Properties (Yoshida et al. 2015)
1. Connectivity effect
A: He is selling pictures of John.
*B: Why pictures of John?
→ Condition C violation: "John" bound by "he" in antecedent carries into remnant
→ Proves the remnant is syntactically connected to the antecedent structure
2. P-Stranding Generalization
A: John was talking to Mary.
B: Why (to) Mary? [English — P-stranding language → P optional]
German: *Warum Abel? → Warum mit Abel? [P-stranding blocked → P obligatory]
3. Island rescue
A: No linguist recommended a book that contains one of his own articles.
B: Why his own articles? ← island violation rescued
B: *Not HIS OWN ARTICLES. ← Not-Stripping does not rescue
